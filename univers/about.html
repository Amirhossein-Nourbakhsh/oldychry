History of Chrysler

After Ricardo Montalban catapulted to well-deserved fame for his Chrysler Cordoba advertisements of the middle 1970s, he starred in the hit television series, "Fantasy Island." That show went off the air after 1984, but Montalban continued to pitch Chryslers for the remainder of the decade. Here we see him pitching the 1984 Fifth Avenue in a full-page magazine advertisement, with a sly reference to his about-to-get-axed show.

Island of Luxury

Sure, the Fifth Avenue was a Dodge Diplomat at heart, but no Diplomat ever had such cushy seats. The Fifth Avenue name started life as the name of an option package on the 1979 Chrysler New Yorker, then became a model name in its own right for 1984 (disappointingly, nobody ever made a car called the Third Avenue). That car was really a luxed-up Dodge Diplomat, a decidedly proletarian machine that mostly performed fleet duties, but just look at those billowy tufted-velour seats! Sadly, it appears that Chrysler didn't use the Corinthian Leather name for the '84 Fifth Avenue, though leather seats were an option.
